Message from the President

David Miliband meets with students in Pakistan, where the IRC and 10 partner organizations are working to improve the quality of reading education. Photo: Umer Farooq/IRC

Dear Friends,

I am honored to present the IRC annual report for 2014, a year in which we provided vital aid to a record 17.6 million vulnerable people around the world, including crucial services for refugees resettling in the United States.

The demand for our work has never been greater, so our response to the challenges we face needs to be increasingly efficient and effective—but also creative. That is why over the past year we have conducted a rigorous, thoughtful and in-depth examination of the way we carry out our work around the world, and how to carry it forward. The result of the review is an ambitious new strategy that will guide our efforts from 2015 through 2020.

Thank you for your interest and support as we write the next chapter of the IRC’s history.

David Miliband

A year of crises

Every day, the IRC works to restore hope and dignity to 17.6 million people in over 40 countries and 23 U.S. cities. In 2014, aid workers faced the worst humanitarian crisis since World War II: Vicious warfare drove millions of people from their homes in the Middle East, Africa and Asia. Meanwhile, the deadly Ebola virus killed thousands in West Africa. The IRC responded by delivering vital aid to these crisis areas without delay, knowing that rapid assistance saves lives.

Ebola in West Africa: Communities fight for survival

West Africa is struggling to stop what has become the largest Ebola outbreak ever recorded. The deadly disease has killed over 10,000 people. In Liberia and Sierra Leone the IRC supports Ebola response efforts on all fronts: treatment, surveillance, aid coordination and community engagement.

Traveling to remote Liberian villages, and speaking the local language, the IRC's Alpha Tamba addressed deadly rumors about Ebola and emphasized that measures such as careful hand-washing and avoiding contact with the dead were extremely effective. Tamba initially encountered resistance. On one occasion he was beaten and driven out of a village. But as more people became sick, his message began to be heard.

In addition to providing medical supplies, protective equipment and training to local health workers, the IRC joined with community leaders to educate people about Ebola and Ebola prevention. Photo: Peter Biro/IRC

Syrian crisis: Cash helps refugees to survive

The four-year-long Syrian conflict has displaced 10 million people, many of whom have sought refuge in neighboring countries. The IRC provides extensive support to refugees and aids thousands of people trapped inside the war-torn country.

IRC aid workers meet with a Syrian refugee who receives cash assistance from the IRC in northern Lebanon. Photo: Rebecca Blum

Lebanon has no refugee camps and Syrian refugees are scattered among 1,200 towns and villages, making them much harder to reach and help. Many live in informal tent settlements and abandoned warehouses or crowd into expensive, overcrowded apartments or the homes of friends. Most can’t afford rent, to buy food and fuel, or pay for schooling for their children. The IRC provides debit cards to thousands of refugees. The cards can be used to withdraw money to spend on necessities of their choice.

“We left Syria with only the clothes we had on,” says Farah, who, following the death of her husband, is now the head of a household of five, all living in a one-room rented apartment in northern Lebanon.

With cash assistance from the IRC, Farah has been able to keep a roof over her family and send her children to a local school. “Debts were accumulating and I didn’t know how we were going to survive,” she recalls. “The IRC visited us at home and they said they were going to help us—and they have.”

South Sudan and Central African Republic: Uprooted by conflict

Conflict between government and militia forces has killed thousands and displaced nearly 2 million people in South Sudan. Sporadic peace talks have failed to end the fighting. The ongoing violence has disrupted planting and harvests, leading to food shortages and hunger. The IRC provides health care, water and sanitation, food and nutrition services, and other aid to people in need.

In the neighboring Central African Republic (CAR), widespread fighting, often along religious lines, put an end to a short-lived period of peace and relative stability. By the end of 2014, thousands of civilians had been killed and over 800,000 people were displaced. The IRC continues to provide essential emergency services in the country despite the high risk to aid workers.

Congo: Scholarships put girls back in the classroom

Some 4 million young girls in the Democratic Republic of Congo — a country beset by years of war and economic decline — will not get an education. Girls who don’t are more likely to marry and have children at a young age, reinforcing the cycle of poverty.

After losing both parents at a very young age, Belbiche Kakadi was forced to drop out of school and work in the fields to support her brothers and sisters. But she has been able to return to school thanks to an IRC-supported scholarship program. School fees alone keep many girls out of the classroom, for when parents must choose which of their children they can afford to educate, boys take precedence. The investment is turning out to be one of the most potent ways to fight poverty.

“Girls want to be useful in society. I hope to become a teacher so I can give other girls this opportunity.”

- Belbiche Kakadi, schoolgirl, Congo

United States: Children at the border, fleeing violence

Throughout its history, the IRC has come to the aid of refugees and asylum seekers fleeing persecution, war and civil conflict. So when some 60,000 unaccompanied children fleeing violence in Central America crossed the U.S. border last summer, the IRC joined the effort to ensure that they were treated humanely and their rights protected.

From the beginning of the crisis, the IRC emphasized that the majority of the children might qualify as refugees and should be guaranteed the right to claim asylum and other protections enshrined in international law. The IRC also helped mobilize public opinion for extending humanitarian protection to children once they are in the U.S.
